# Immortal Technique

## The Man

**Born:** Felipe Andres Coronel, February 19, 1978, Lima, Peru
**Raised:** Harlem, New York City
**Heritage:** Peruvian-American — Afro-Peruvian and Indigenous ancestry
**Label:** Viper Records (his own — founded 2003)
**Education:** Studied political science at Baruch College (CUNY)

## Background

Felipe Coronel was born in Lima during a period of military rule and political instability in Peru. His family emigrated to the United States when he was a child, settling in Harlem, New York. Growing up in Harlem in the late '80s and early '90s — during the crack epidemic — shaped the political consciousness that would define his entire body of work.

As a teenager, Technique was involved in street life. He caught a charge for assault and did time in correctional facilities in Pennsylvania. Prison became his university. He read voraciously — Marx, Chomsky, Frantz Fanon, Eduardo Galeano, Howard Zinn. He came out radicalized, educated, and focused. He channeled the anger and intelligence into rap.

He studied political science at Baruch College but left before completing his degree — choosing to pursue music full-time. He built his reputation the hardest way possible: battle rapping in Harlem, selling CDs hand-to-hand on the streets of New York, and performing anywhere that would have him.

## The Independent Grind

Technique never signed a major label deal. He had offers — serious ones — and turned them all down. His reasoning was simple and absolute: creative control is non-negotiable. A major label would dilute the message. The music exists to tell the truth, not to sell units.

He founded **Viper Records** in 2003 as his own independent label. Every release has come through Viper. He handles his own distribution, his own promotion, his own business. He proved that an artist with an uncompromising political message could build a real career — selling hundreds of thousands of records — without ever shaking hands with the machine.

This is the same model Danny is building with Equality Music. No middlemen. No gatekeepers. Own everything. Say whatever needs to be said.

## Discography

### Revolutionary Vol. 1 (2001)

Self-produced, self-distributed debut. Sold hand-to-hand on the streets of Harlem and through underground channels. The album announced Technique as the most politically charged voice in underground hip hop. Tracks like "Industrial Revolution" laid out his worldview with surgical precision — connecting imperialism, capitalism, racism, and class warfare into a unified indictment of the system.

"Industrial Revolution" is the track Danny covers a cappella on the **Cover Up** album. The song is a three-part series across the first two albums — each part escalating in scope and intensity.

### Revolutionary Vol. 2 (2003)

The album that made his name impossible to ignore. Released on Viper Records. Sold over 100,000 copies independently — with no radio play, no major label marketing, no MTV.

**"Dance With The Devil"** is the centerpiece. A narrative track that tells the story of a young man named Billy Jacobs who, in a desperate attempt to prove himself to a gang, participates in the rape and murder of a woman who turns out to be his own mother. The song ends with Billy jumping off the roof. Technique reveals he was there — that this story is a confession, not fiction.

It is one of the most disturbing and powerful songs in hip hop history. The point is not shock value. The point is that the cycle of poverty, violence, and desperation destroys people from the inside out — and that the system that creates these conditions is the real devil you are dancing with.

Other essential tracks: "Peruvian Cocaine" (tracing the cocaine supply chain from Peruvian coca fields to American streets), "Harlem Streets" (with the hook sampled from Tupac), "The Cause of Death" (a post-9/11 political manifesto connecting American foreign policy to domestic suffering), "Industrial Revolution Part 2."

### The 3rd World (2008)

Expanded the lens from American inner cities to global politics. Technique traveled to the Middle East, Africa, and Latin America — recording on location and incorporating sounds and perspectives from the places he visited. The album connected the struggles of the Global South to the same systems of exploitation he had been rapping about since Vol. 1.

### The Martyr (2011)

Released as a free mixtape — his gift to the fans. Featured collaborations with Killer Mike, Brother Ali, and others. Continued the political analysis with tracks addressing the War on Drugs, media manipulation, and economic inequality.

### Revolutionary Vol. 3 (Forthcoming)

The most anticipated album in underground hip hop. Technique has spoken about it for years. Its delay is itself a statement — he refuses to release it until it meets his standard.

## Political Consciousness

Technique is not a rapper who happens to be political. He is a political thinker who uses rap as his medium. His analysis is rooted in:

- **Anti-imperialism** — connecting U.S. foreign policy to the destabilization of Latin America, the Middle East, and Africa
- **Class consciousness** — the exploitation of the poor by the wealthy is the through-line of every album
- **Racial justice** — but always connected to economic systems, never isolated as identity politics
- **Latin American solidarity** — proud of his Peruvian heritage, vocal about the history of U.S. intervention in Central and South America
- **Media literacy** — consistent critiques of how corporate media manufactures consent and keeps people divided

He does not align neatly with any American political party. He has been critical of both Republicans and Democrats. His politics are rooted in liberation movements, not electoral cycles.

## Community Work

Technique puts his money and his body where his mouth is. He has:

- Built an **orphanage in Afghanistan** — funded through record sales and donations, constructed in Kabul to house children orphaned by the war
- Organized and funded **community projects in Harlem** — working with local organizations on housing, education, and youth programs
- Traveled to **Peru, Afghanistan, and other countries** to document conditions on the ground and connect with local communities
- Supported **political prisoners** and prison reform causes
- Donated proceeds from sales and performances to grassroots organizations

This is not performative activism. Technique builds things. He shows up. He stays.

## Why He Matters to Danny / '89 Vision

### The Rebel Mission

Danny's mission statement: "Change the world like Bob. We are rebels and we won't sell out." Immortal Technique is one of the purest examples of that mission in hip hop. He had every opportunity to cash in and chose not to. He had every reason to soften his message and refused. He built his own infrastructure and kept 100% ownership of his work. That is exactly what Equality Music is being built to do.

### The Independent Blueprint

Technique sold hundreds of thousands of records with no major label, no radio, no mainstream press. He proved the model works. Danny is walking the same path — releasing everything independently through eMastered, owning the masters, keeping the message intact.

### The Cover

Danny covers "Industrial Revolution" a cappella on the **Cover Up** album ('89 Vision). That is not a casual choice. "Industrial Revolution" is a song about systems of control — about how the music industry, the prison system, the war machine, and corporate media all serve the same masters. Danny choosing to perform those words with nothing but his voice is a statement: the truth does not need a beat behind it. The words are enough.

### Truth-Telling DNA

Technique and Danny share the same core drive — expose the truth, no matter the cost. Technique does it through political analysis. Danny does it through personal testimony. Different angles, same refusal to lie.

### The Politics of Equality

Equality Music's mission comes from 2 Corinthians — "that there might be equality." Technique's entire catalog is built on the same idea: the world is structured to benefit the few at the expense of the many, and the only moral response is to fight for balance. His Peruvian-American identity, his Harlem upbringing, his global travels — all of it feeds a worldview that says every culture, every people, every community deserves dignity. That is what Equality Music represents.

### From the Bottom

Technique grew up poor in Harlem. Danny grew up in the projects in San Diego. Both went to jail. Both educated themselves. Both chose to build rather than destroy. Both refused to let the system define them. The parallel is not a coincidence — it is the shared experience of people who come from nothing and decide that "nothing" is not the end of the story.
